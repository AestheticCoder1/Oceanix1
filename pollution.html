Plastic Debris

There are 25 trillion pieces of plastic debris in the ocean.
Of that, 269,000 tons float on the surface, while some four billion plastic
microfibers per square kilometer litter the deep sea.

Dead Zones in the ocean

There are dead zones in the oceans that have been created by pollution
making life in those zones impossible for marine or plant life
There are about 500 dead zones in the ocean, which covers a similar size to the United Kingdom.

Carbon Emmisions which effect the ocean

Carbon emissions harm the oceans as well as the air.
If our behavior continues as is, the surface water of the
ocean could be 150% more acidic than it is now.

Oil Spills

Oil spills only contribute to 12% of the oil in the ocean.
36% of the oil comes from runoff sources from cities and
companies.

Many “flavors” of marine pollution

Marine pollution, as distinct from overall water pollution, focuses on human-created products that enter the ocean. Before 1972, humans around the word spewed trash, sewage sludge, and chemical, industrial, and radioactive wastes into the ocean with impunity. Millions of tons of heavy metals and chemical contaminants, along with thousands of containers of radioactive waste, were purposely thrown into the ocean.

Even nutrients can become harmful.

When dumped at sea in large amounts, agricultural nutrients such as nitrogen can stimulate the explosive growth of algae. When the algae decomposes, oxygen in the surrounding waters is consumed, creating a vast dead zone that can result in mass die-offs of fish and other marine life.

​The oceans are losing mussel mass.

One effect of greenhouse emissions is increased ocean acidification, which makes it more difficult for bivalves such as mussels, clams and oysters to form shells, decreasing their likelihood of survival, upsetting the food chain and impacting the multibillion-dollar shellfish industry.
